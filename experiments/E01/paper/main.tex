\begin{abstract}
Autonomous research systems depend on accurate extraction of factual claims
from scientific papers to build reliable knowledge graphs.  We evaluate five
large language models---Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4,
GPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Haiku---on the task of extracting verifiable claims
from machine-learning paper abstracts.  Using a dual-annotator ground-truth
protocol and embedding-based claim matching (cosine similarity threshold
0.85), we measure per-model precision, recall, F1, and hallucination rate.
Our best-performing model, Claude Sonnet 4, achieves a precision of 0.803
(95\% CI [0.756, 0.848]), recall of 0.660 (95\% CI [0.615, 0.705]), and F1
of 0.720 (95\% CI [0.677, 0.762]), with a hallucination rate of 0.213
(95\% CI [0.184, 0.245]).  Notably, the most capable model tested (Opus
4.6, F1 = 0.612) and its newer sibling (Sonnet 4.6, F1 = 0.589) do not
achieve the highest extraction accuracy.  We test three pre-registered
hypotheses (precision $\geq 0.80$, recall $\geq 0.70$, hallucination rate
$\leq 0.10$) and report bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals and pairwise
McNemar tests.  None of the three hypotheses are supported, indicating that
LLM-based claim extraction requires additional verification layers before
deployment in autonomous research pipelines.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Building reliable knowledge graphs from scientific literature is a
prerequisite for autonomous AI research systems that must reason over an
evolving body of evidence~\citep{lu2024aiscientist}.  The first step in
such a pipeline is \emph{claim extraction}: given a paper (or its
abstract), produce a set of discrete, verifiable factual statements.

Errors at this stage propagate downstream: a spurious claim (hallucination)
can trigger wasted experiments, while a missed claim creates blind spots
in the literature review.  Quantifying these failure modes across frontier
language models is therefore essential before deploying an autonomous
research agent.

In this work we design a controlled experiment (Experiment~E01) that
measures the precision, recall, F1, and hallucination rate of five LLMs
on claim extraction from ML paper abstracts.  We contribute:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item A dual-annotator ground-truth corpus of claims extracted from
        18 ArXiv abstracts, with reconciliation and inter-annotator
        agreement statistics.
  \item A systematic comparison of Claude Opus 4.6, Claude Sonnet 4.6,
        Claude Sonnet 4, GPT-4o, and Claude 3.5 Haiku on this corpus.
  \item Pre-registered hypothesis tests with bootstrap confidence intervals,
        McNemar pairwise comparisons, and effect-size estimates.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{LLM-as-Judge.}
\citet{zheng2023judging} demonstrate that strong LLMs can serve as reliable
evaluators of open-ended text generation, achieving high agreement with
human raters.  We extend this paradigm to structured claim extraction,
where the evaluation target is a \emph{set} of propositions rather than a
single response.

\paragraph{AI Scientist.}
\citet{lu2024aiscientist} present an end-to-end autonomous research agent
capable of ideation, experimentation, and paper writing.  Their system
relies on accurate literature understanding but does not isolate claim
extraction as a measurable subtask.  Our experiment fills this gap by
providing quantitative baselines.

\paragraph{Information extraction from scientific papers.}
Prior work on scientific information extraction has focused on named-entity
recognition~\citep{beltagy2019scibert}, relation extraction, and citation
intent classification.  Claim-level extraction---identifying self-contained
factual assertions---is less studied, although recent work on fact
verification~\citep{wadden2020fact} addresses the complementary problem of
checking claims against evidence.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Paper Selection}
\label{sec:paper-selection}

We sample 18 abstracts from the ArXiv preprint server using stratified
sampling across 4 machine-learning subcategories (cs.LG, cs.CL, cs.AI,
stat.ML).  Papers are drawn from March 2026 and filtered to ensure a
minimum abstract length of 100 words.

\subsection{Ground Truth Annotation}
\label{sec:annotation}

Two independent annotators (LLM-based, using Claude Opus 4 via
OpenRouter---a separate model instance from those under evaluation) extract
claims from each abstract under two annotation strategies:

\begin{description}
  \item[Conservative:] Only claims that are explicitly and unambiguously
    stated in the text.  Designed to maximize precision.
  \item[Comprehensive:] All claims, including those reasonably implied by
    the text.  Designed to maximize recall.
\end{description}

We reconcile the two annotation sets by taking the union: agreed claims
(identified via Jaccard token similarity $\geq 0.40$) plus
comprehensive-only claims form the gold standard.  The mean agreement rate
across papers is 0.33, reflecting the deliberate asymmetry of the two
annotation policies.  The resulting gold standard contains an average of
16.6 claims per paper (range: 11--27), totaling 282 claims across 17
usable papers (one paper yielded 0 claims due to annotation parse
failure).

\subsection{Models Tested}
\label{sec:models}

We evaluate five models accessed via the OpenRouter API:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Claude Opus 4.6} (\texttt{anthropic/claude-opus-4-6}).
  \item \textbf{Claude Sonnet 4.6} (\texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4-6}).
  \item \textbf{Claude Sonnet 4} (\texttt{anthropic/claude-sonnet-4}).
  \item \textbf{GPT-4o} (\texttt{openai/gpt-4o}).
  \item \textbf{Claude 3.5 Haiku} (\texttt{anthropic/claude-3.5-haiku}).
\end{enumerate}

Each model receives the same system prompt and paper abstract.  We perform
3 independent runs per model per paper (temperature = 0) to assess
extraction stability, yielding $18 \times 5 \times 3 = 270$ extraction
runs total.

\subsection{Claim Matching}
\label{sec:matching}

Extracted claims are matched to ground-truth claims using embedding cosine
similarity.  We embed all claims with OpenAI's
\texttt{text-embedding-3-small} model and define a match when the cosine
similarity exceeds a threshold of $\tau = 0.85$.  Matching is performed
via greedy one-to-one assignment: extracted claims are sorted by their best
similarity score, and each ground-truth claim is matched to at most one
extracted claim.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{True positive (TP):} extracted claim matches a
        ground-truth claim ($\mathrm{sim} \geq 0.85$).
  \item \textbf{False positive (FP):} extracted claim has no match
        (potential hallucination).
  \item \textbf{False negative (FN):} ground-truth claim has no match.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical Tests}
\label{sec:statistics}

We report the following statistics:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Bootstrap 95\% CIs} ($B = 10{,}000$) for precision,
        recall, and F1 per model~\citep{efron1993bootstrap}.
  \item \textbf{McNemar's test} for pairwise comparison of model accuracy
        on matched claim pairs~\citep{mcnemar1947note}.
  \item \textbf{Binomial 95\% CI} for hallucination rate.
  \item \textbf{Cohen's $d$} for effect size between model pairs on F1.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Hypothesis Evaluation}

We pre-registered three hypotheses for the best-performing model:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{H1 (Precision $\geq 0.80$):}
    \textbf{Marginally supported}---the observed precision for Sonnet 4 is
    0.803 (95\% CI [0.756, 0.848]).  The point estimate just exceeds
    the threshold, though the lower CI bound falls below it.
  \item \textbf{H2 (Recall $\geq 0.70$):}
    \textbf{Not supported}---the observed recall is
    0.660 (95\% CI [0.615, 0.705]).  The upper bound of the CI just
    reaches the threshold but the point estimate is well below.
  \item \textbf{H3 (Hallucination rate $\leq 0.10$):}
    \textbf{Not supported}---the observed hallucination rate is
    0.213 (95\% binomial CI [0.184, 0.245]).  This is more than double
    the target threshold, indicating a substantial rate of fabricated claims.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Key Findings}

\paragraph{Sonnet 4 outperforms both Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6 on structured extraction.}
A surprising finding is that the older Claude Sonnet 4 (F1 = 0.720)
outperforms both Claude Opus 4.6 (F1 = 0.612) and the newer Claude
Sonnet 4.6 (F1 = 0.589) on this task, despite both being more capable
models in general benchmarks.  Sonnet 4.6 is particularly notable: it
achieves the highest recall among all models except Sonnet 4 (0.605) but
the lowest precision (0.649) and highest hallucination rate (0.388),
suggesting it over-generates claims.  None of the pairwise differences
among these three Anthropic frontier models reach significance (all
$p > 0.05$), but the Cohen's $d$ between Sonnet 4.6 and Sonnet 4
($d = -0.727$) indicates a medium-to-large effect.  These results suggest
that newer or more capable models are not always optimal for structured
extraction tasks.

\paragraph{Model ranking is largely consistent.}
Across all metrics, the ranking is Sonnet 4 $>$ Opus 4.6 $>$ Sonnet 4.6
$>$ GPT-4o $>$ Haiku.  GPT-4o achieves precision comparable to Sonnet 4
(0.773 vs.\ 0.803) but substantially lower recall (0.418 vs.\ 0.660),
suggesting it applies a more conservative extraction strategy.  Sonnet 4.6
breaks this pattern by trading precision for recall relative to Opus 4.6,
achieving slightly better recall (0.605 vs.\ 0.551) but notably worse
precision (0.649 vs.\ 0.703).

\paragraph{Recall is the primary bottleneck.}
All models exhibit higher precision than recall, suggesting they tend to
extract a conservative subset of claims rather than hallucinating
extensively.  Sonnet 4.6 is the most prolific extractor (15.3 claims per
paper on average), followed by Sonnet 4 (13.5), Opus (12.1), GPT-4o
(8.4), and Haiku (6.6), compared to a gold standard average of 16.6
claims.

\paragraph{Hallucination rates are concerning across all models.}
Even the best model produces hallucinated claims 21\% of the time.  This
suggests that any autonomous pipeline using LLM claim extraction must
include a downstream verification step to filter spurious claims.  Sonnet
4.6 is the worst offender at 39\%, indicating that its higher extraction
volume comes at a significant cost to precision.

\paragraph{High extraction stability.}
With temperature set to 0, all five models produced nearly identical
claim sets across the 3 independent runs, confirming that extraction
variability is not a significant concern for deterministic inference.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations qualify these results:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Abstract-only text.}  We extract claims from abstracts
    rather than full papers.  Abstracts are information-dense but omit
    methodological nuance; performance may differ on full-text input.
  \item \textbf{LLM-based ground truth.}  Our ground truth is produced by
    an LLM annotator (Claude Opus 4) rather than domain-expert humans.
    While the dual-annotator reconciliation protocol mitigates individual
    model bias, systematic blind spots shared across LLMs may persist.
    The comprehensive annotator dominates the gold standard (mean agreement
    rate 0.33), which may inflate recall denominators.
  \item \textbf{Embedding-based matching.}  Cosine similarity at threshold
    0.85 may miss semantically equivalent claims that differ substantially
    in phrasing.  A lower threshold would increase matches but also
    increase false matches.
  \item \textbf{Sample size.}  With 18 papers (17 usable), statistical
    power for detecting small effects is limited.  The bootstrap CIs
    reflect this uncertainty.
  \item \textbf{Annotation model overlap.}  The ground truth annotator
    (Opus 4) is from the same model family as three of the models under
    evaluation (Opus 4.6, Sonnet 4.6, and Sonnet 4).  This could introduce
    systematic bias favoring Anthropic models, though the consistent GPT-4o
    results suggest this is not a dominant effect.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implications for Downstream Experiments}

The failure to meet all three hypothesis thresholds has direct implications
for the design of the autonomous research pipeline.  First, claim
extraction cannot be treated as a solved problem: any downstream module
(knowledge graph construction, hypothesis generation) must account for a
$\sim$22\% hallucination rate.  Second, the recall gap means that
$\sim$35\% of true claims are missed, requiring either multi-pass
extraction or complementary retrieval strategies.

Based on these results, subsequent experiments in the autonomous research
pipeline (E02 and beyond) should adopt Claude Sonnet 4 as the default
claim-extraction backbone, with an expected precision floor of
approximately 0.75 and recall floor of approximately 0.61.  A claim
verification module (E02) becomes essential rather than optional.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a controlled evaluation of five frontier LLMs on the task of
extracting factual claims from ML paper abstracts.  Using a dual-annotator
ground-truth corpus and embedding-based matching, we measured precision,
recall, F1, and hallucination rates with bootstrap confidence intervals
and pairwise statistical tests.

Claude Sonnet 4 achieved the highest overall F1 of 0.720, outperforming
the newer Sonnet 4.6 (F1 = 0.589), the more capable Opus 4.6
(F1 = 0.612), GPT-4o (F1 = 0.518), and Haiku (F1 = 0.386).  Of three
pre-registered hypotheses, only precision marginally passed (0.803 vs.\
threshold 0.80); recall was substantially below 0.70, and the
hallucination rate more than doubled the 0.10 threshold.  Most pairwise
model differences were statistically significant ($p < 0.05$) with large
effect sizes, except among the three Anthropic frontier models (Opus 4.6,
Sonnet 4.6, and Sonnet 4).

The finding that model capability and recency do not monotonically predict
extraction accuracy suggests that task-specific evaluation is essential
when selecting models for structured information extraction in autonomous
research systems.  These baselines inform the design of subsequent modules,
particularly the claim verification experiment (E02), which these results
indicate is essential for reliable knowledge graph construction.
