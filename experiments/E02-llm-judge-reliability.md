# E02: LLM Judge Reliability for Research Hypothesis Evaluation

> **Layer 1 — Foundational Capabilities**

## Status: Planned

## Hypothesis

LLM judges can evaluate AI research hypothesis quality with Cohen's kappa >= 0.4 (moderate agreement) against expert human ratings on at least 3 of 5 quality dimensions, and Spearman rank correlation rho >= 0.5 for overall quality, when using structured rubrics.

This hypothesis is falsifiable: if kappa < 0.2 on a majority of dimensions, the hypothesis is rejected and automated evaluation cannot be trusted for optimization loops.

## Why This Matters

Automated evaluation is the single most critical unsolved problem across all system documents. Every optimization loop (SUNFIRE, MAP-Elites, meta-RL, self-play) requires a quality signal to determine whether a generated hypothesis is better or worse than alternatives. If LLM judges do not correlate with human expert judgment, all optimization loops will Goodhart — optimizing a proxy metric that diverges from actual research quality. This experiment determines whether automated evaluation is viable at all, or whether human-in-the-loop evaluation is mandatory at every iteration.

## Background & Prior Work

**LLM-as-Judge.** Zheng et al. (2023) established that GPT-4 agrees with human preferences at 85%, exceeding human-human agreement at 81%, on general conversational quality. However, they identified three systematic biases: position bias (preference for the first option in pairwise comparisons), verbosity bias (preference for longer responses), and self-preference bias (models rating their own outputs higher). Cohen's kappa on code evaluation was substantially lower (~0.21 for Java, ~0.10 for Python), suggesting domain-dependent reliability.

**AI Scientist automated review.** Lu et al. (2024) found their automated reviewer achieved accuracy of 0.65 (vs 0.66 for humans) on accept/reject decisions, with correlation r=0.18 with average human scores (compared to r=0.14 for human-human correlation). F1 was 0.57 for the automated reviewer vs 0.49 for humans at a threshold of 6. These results are encouraging but based on a specific review format; generalization to hypothesis evaluation is untested.

**Structured evaluation.** Pairwise comparison formats generally produce more reliable judgments than absolute scoring (Zheng et al., 2023). Chain-of-thought prompting can improve consistency but may also amplify systematic biases. Both formats must be tested.

**Statistical context.** Cohen's kappa of 0.4 represents "moderate" agreement per Landis & Koch (1977). In peer review, inter-reviewer agreement is notoriously low — Bornmann et al. (2010) found kappa values of 0.17-0.34 across multiple studies. Our target of 0.4 is therefore ambitious relative to human-human baselines but necessary for practical use.

## Methodology

### Design

Mixed design with two factors:

1. **Judge type** (between-groups): 3 human experts vs 3 LLM judges (Claude Opus, GPT-4o, Gemini Pro).
2. **Evaluation format** (within-subjects): absolute scoring (1-7 Likert per dimension) vs pairwise comparison (which hypothesis is better on each dimension), crossed with chain-of-thought (present/absent).

This yields a 2 x 2 within-subjects design for format, with the primary comparison being human-LLM agreement.

### Data Requirements

- **Hypotheses:** 50 research hypotheses generated by the hypothesis generator agent across varied quality levels. Stratified as approximately 10 trivial/obvious, 10 incremental, 10 ambitious/novel, 10 deliberately flawed (logical errors, unfalsifiable, or circular), and 10 mixed quality. Each hypothesis includes a 2-3 sentence description and a brief rationale.
- **Human ratings:** 3 domain experts in AI/ML research, each rating all 50 hypotheses on 5 dimensions. Total: 750 individual ratings.
- **LLM ratings:** 3 LLM judges, each rating all 50 hypotheses on 5 dimensions, in both absolute and pairwise formats, with and without chain-of-thought. Total: at least 750 ratings (absolute) plus pairwise comparisons for 50 choose 2 = 1,225 pairs per model.
- **Quality dimensions:** (1) Novelty: does this propose something not already established? (2) Feasibility: can this be tested with available methods and resources? (3) Importance: would a positive result matter to the field? (4) Clarity: is the hypothesis precisely stated and unambiguous? (5) Specificity: does it make concrete, measurable predictions?

### Procedure

1. **Hypothesis generation.** Generate 50 hypotheses using the hypothesis generator agent. Manually verify stratification across quality levels. Include source attribution (which model generated each hypothesis) but blind this during evaluation.
2. **Rubric development.** Develop detailed scoring rubrics for each of the 5 dimensions with anchor examples at scores 1, 4, and 7. Pilot with 2 experts on 5 hypotheses. Revise rubric based on disagreements.
3. **Human evaluation.** Each of 3 experts independently rates all 50 hypotheses. Order is randomized independently for each expert. Collect absolute scores (1-7 per dimension) and overall quality ranking. Measure time per hypothesis.
4. **LLM evaluation — absolute scoring.** Each of 3 LLM judges rates all 50 hypotheses using the same rubric. Run with and without chain-of-thought. Use temperature 0. Randomize presentation order across 3 runs to measure position bias.
5. **LLM evaluation — pairwise comparison.** For a subset of 20 hypothesis pairs (selected to span the quality range), each LLM judge evaluates which is better on each dimension. Present each pair in both orders (A-B and B-A) to measure position bias directly.
6. **Self-preference bias test.** Include 10 hypotheses generated by Claude, 10 by GPT-4, and 10 by Gemini (included within the 50). After evaluation, test whether each LLM judge rates hypotheses from its own model family significantly higher than those from other models.
7. **Agreement analysis.** Compute all metrics (see below). Compare human-LLM agreement to human-human agreement to contextualize results.

### Metrics & Statistical Tests

| Metric | Definition | Statistical Test | Justification |
|--------|-----------|-----------------|---------------|
| Cohen's kappa (per dimension) | Agreement between each LLM judge and each human expert, averaged | Bootstrap 95% CI (10,000 resamples over hypotheses) | Standard chance-corrected agreement; bootstrap because kappa distribution is complex |
| Spearman rho (overall) | Rank correlation between mean LLM score and mean human score | Permutation test (10,000 permutations) for p-value | Non-parametric rank correlation; permutation test avoids normality assumption |
| Position bias | Mean score difference when hypothesis is presented first vs later | Wilcoxon signed-rank test (paired, non-normal) | Paired comparison on same hypotheses in different positions; Wilcoxon because score differences are unlikely to be normal |
| Self-preference bias | Mean score of own-model hypotheses minus other-model hypotheses | Mann-Whitney U test (independent groups) | Independent samples (own vs other); Mann-Whitney because Likert data is ordinal |
| Pairwise vs absolute agreement | Kappa using pairwise-derived rankings vs absolute scores | Descriptive comparison | Determines which format is more reliable |
| Chain-of-thought effect | Kappa difference with/without CoT | Bootstrap 95% CI on difference | Determines whether CoT helps or hurts |
| Human-human agreement | Cohen's kappa between all human expert pairs | Point estimate with SE | Baseline: LLM-human agreement should approach human-human |

**Effect sizes:** Report Cohen's d for bias tests. Position bias or self-preference bias is considered meaningful only if d >= 0.3 (small-to-medium effect) AND statistically significant.

**Multiple comparisons:** With 5 dimensions tested, apply Bonferroni correction (alpha = 0.01 per dimension) when testing whether kappa >= 0.4 threshold is met.

## Success Criteria

- Cohen's kappa >= 0.4 between the best LLM judge and human experts on at least 3 of 5 dimensions (Bonferroni-corrected).
- Spearman rho >= 0.5 for overall quality ranking (best LLM judge vs mean human ranking).
- Position bias effect size d < 0.3 (negligible), OR a debiasing method reduces it below 0.3.
- Self-preference bias effect size d < 0.3, OR a debiasing method reduces it below 0.3.

## Failure Criteria

- Kappa < 0.2 on a majority (3+) of dimensions for all LLM judges. This would indicate LLM evaluation is essentially uncorrelated with human judgment after chance correction, making automated optimization loops unreliable.
- Spearman rho < 0.3 for all models. Overall rankings would be too noisy to guide hypothesis selection.
- Self-preference bias d > 0.5 with no effective debiasing. This would compromise any system using the same model for generation and evaluation.

**If the experiment fails:** Consider (1) pairwise comparison only (which Zheng et al. found more reliable), (2) panel-of-judges averaging to reduce noise, (3) calibration against known-quality anchors, (4) dimension reduction (some dimensions may be reliably judged while others are not), or (5) accepting human-in-the-loop as mandatory for quality-critical evaluations. Partial failure (some dimensions reliable, others not) would still be informative — the system could use LLM judges for reliable dimensions and flag others for human review.

## Estimated Cost & Timeline

- **API calls:** ~$100-200 (50 hypotheses x 3 models x multiple formats x multiple runs)
- **Human expert time:** ~10-20 hours across 3 experts (50 hypotheses at 3-5 min/hypothesis per expert)
- **Expert recruitment:** May require compensation; budget $500-1,000 for expert time
- **Analysis and write-up:** ~8-12 hours
- **Calendar time:** 3-4 weeks (human expert scheduling is the bottleneck)

## Dependencies

None. This is a foundational experiment with no upstream dependencies. However, the hypothesis generator agent should be functional to produce the 50 test hypotheses.

## Informs

- **E06** (SUNFIRE): The SUNFIRE loop uses LLM judges for hypothesis scoring at every iteration. This experiment determines whether that signal is trustworthy.
- **E09-E14** (all loop experiments): Every optimization loop relies on automated evaluation. The specific format (pairwise vs absolute, with/without CoT) and bias mitigations identified here will be adopted across all experiments.
- **System design:** If LLM judges fail entirely, the system architecture must be redesigned around human-in-the-loop evaluation, fundamentally changing throughput and cost assumptions.
